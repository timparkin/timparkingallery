Simon Miles said...

Personally, I cannot justify the cost of a high-end scanner, like a Flextight, because I just do not shoot anywhere near enough images per year to recoup the capital cost. Equally, I do not have the time to mess about with a drum scanner.

I therefore get David at BlueSkyImages to scan my trannies on his Imacon. For £12 per scan, I get a 400MB 16-bit scan that is perfectly colour balanced to the original tranny, scanned exactly to my instructions and meticulously cleaned by hand of all dust. The cleaning alone is worth the cost, because my time can be spent much more profitably doing my "proper job." There are a lot of photographers offering similar services, who generally take a lot more care over their scans than the larger pro or pro-am labs, and I highly recommend this option.

As to the quality, I agree that a drum scan is better, in absolute terms. However, in my experience, this is only really visible when viewing at 100% magnification on a computer screen. When it comes to making prints, even large format prints, I think the difference is barely perceptible and, for me, not worth the significant extra cost of buying a drum scan or the hassle of running one of my own.
(I should say that my comments apply to 5x4inch film, which has huge reserves of quality in the first place; the situation would no doubt be different for smaller film formats).

Others will, of course, have different priorities and opinions, and may simply prefer to make their own scans, rather than rely on a third party. But wouldn't it be boring if we were all the same...

Tim Parkin said...

Hi Mike,

The Howtek max resolution is 4000dpi however the realistic max resolution from 4x5 Velvia is about 3,500 which still equates to a 750Mb file.

The 'standard' medium format film scanner is the Nikon 9000 which scans at 4000dpi - this is the 800Mb equivalent (and the resolution of the scans in the comparison).

If I were scanning for archiving (i.e. with the possibility of doing a large fine art print in the future) I wouldn't want to do so below 3000dpi; would probably scan at 4000dpi and then downrez - My thinking being that the only difference is time if you have your own scanner and storage is insignificant compared with effort etc).

The 200Mb file you mention would be a 2000dpi file which, whilst fine for most uses, would be missing a bit if you wanted to produce large prints. Of course the chances of needing a 30"x40" file is minimal but if you do, and you want to prnt at epson's print native resolution (360dpi) then you want a 450Mb file ($110).

Saying that, if I were paying for scans at the rates that West Coast are charging, 200Mb would probably be the most practical size.. it would make an excellent 20x24 and an acceptable 30x40 (240dpi).

I forgot to mention that you don't actually get 4x5" (96mmx120mm) and with clip marks (etc) you only really get 90x110mm so you need 15% extra linear resolution to cope with this loss - so for a 360 dpi print you'd need 2500dpi for a 24x30
or 3200dpi for a 30x40

Bottom line - 2000dpi is fine but I'd scan at 4000dpi if I had a choice. I'd only do this for 3 or 4 a month and then Epson the rest.

[Photo]

I've posted previously about a comparison between drum scanners and Epson flatbeds. The fact that the drum scanner trounced the Epson was not really in contention but recently I had the opportunity to get a scan from a modern Imacon scanner (owned by Joe Cornish) and from a Howtek 4000 drum scanner (re-badged Crosfield Magnascan 200i I believe, and owned by Dav Thomas) using Silverfast Ai v 6.6 & Kami SXL 2001 fluid both using the same transparency that I had previously had problems with using my Epson.

I knew that the drum scanner was going to provide a better resolution scan than the Imacon but I was more interested in how well it scanned shadows and was it immune to some of the artefacts that I'd seen from the Imacon and the Epson. These artefacts were halation and chromatic abberation.

Halation, a glow around high brightness areas that impinges into darker areas. I have seen a small version of this, a few pixels on a 2000dpi scan, and also a larger version that spreads 2-300 pixels. In photographic terms this is similar to flare and I imagine it occurs in a similar fashion. In the example scan you can see this in the area around the cliff edge and also at the top of the worms head island.

Interactive Example of Halation

Chromatic abberation is evinced as a red and blue borders at opposite sides of a high contrast edge. In the scan you can see this the worst around the white lichen hot spots at the bottom left of the picture but it can also be seen at the top right of the cliff top and also as a strange colouring in the sea where you have a highlight off the wave top right next to a shadow from that same wave. The image at the top of this post shows one of an example of this.

Interactive example of Chromatic Abberation

In comparing these results, it should be kept in mind that the drum scan took 5 mins of mounting and 1.5 hours of scanning wheras the Imacon took 5 minutes to do both.

Here is one of the clearer examples which shows what happens if you want to pull detail out of shadow areas..

Interactive Example of pulling shadow detail - look closeley at the twig bottom left

Click on the many links on the left hand side of the Interactive Example to see more crops.

In conclusion it seems that the best way to scan images is to get a drum scanner. However, there are many other factors at play here. Wet scanning is messy and a lot of people are uncomfortable exposing transparencies in this way. Also, the whole process is a lot more time consuming. Finally, the amount of room a drum scanner takes up can be 10x as much as an Imacon. Realistically, if you want to scan more than a couple of photos a month and you have the budget, an Imacon will always be a better choice. However, for those with limited budgets, excess time and who only process a few images a month, a drum scanner will produce stunning results.
